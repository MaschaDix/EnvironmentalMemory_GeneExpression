## Abstract
Rising ocean temperatures are threatening the persistence of coral reef ecosystems. Thermal preconditioning can improve resilience to subsequent heat stress in corals and other cnidarians, yet the regulatory mechanisms underlying this acquired thermotolerance remain poorly understood. Here, we present a transcriptional phenotyping approach to investigate whether different thermal histories induce distinct transcriptional response strategies during acute heat stress using the symbiotic sea anemone Exaiptasia diaphana. Clonal anemones were subjected to three preconditioning regimes, short-term priming (1-week exposure to 32°C followed by 1-week recovery at 25°C), long-term priming (> 3 years exposure to 32°C followed by 1-week recovery at 25°C), and long-term acclimation (> 3 years exposure to 32°C, no recovery), as well as a naïve control (maintained at 25°C), before exposure to acute heat stress at 34°C. All preconditioned treatments showed improved physiological performance relative to naïve anemones, although the extent and duration of protection differed among treatments. Acclimated anemones displayed the lowest overall transcriptional plasticity, whereas long-term primed anemones exhibited significantly elevated plasticity and priming-type transcriptional responses. One co-expression module enriched for ATP synthesis and proton transport processes showed a positive correlation between transcriptional plasticity and symbiont retention, indicating that elevated plasticity may, in some cases, accompany beneficial stress responses. In contrast, acclimated and short-term primed treatments frequently exhibited lower-plasticity type responses, consistent with previous observations linking transcriptional stability to stress resilience. Together, our findings demonstrate that distinct thermal histories can generate alternative transcriptional phenotypes within a single genotype, and that the regulatory route to improved performance differs with thermal history.
